The Track

The track is short yet fairly diverse. There are many different challenges throughout. The 2 most difficult Corners tend to be Turn 3 and Turn 5. turn 3 is a tight uphill turn with a grade reversal on exit.

Analysis

Here is an indepth breakdown of line choices, g forces, and techniques. The turns with the greatest time deficits are depicted below.

Overview

Here we have 2 drivers, green and red. Green is our faster time. Turn 5 is a long full 180 degree turn. it requires good balance and trajectories. it is inherently harder to get through the corner on a wrong line.

Line Choice

A large portion of getting better is line choice. Red enters the corner at a faster speed yet loses time through the corner in comparison to green. Green stays outside entering the corner allowing him to maintain speed and exit quicker. Doing this also lets him turn in sooner. this is marked by the arrows in the image. reds later turn in and tighter overall radius forces the driver to slow down to make the corner and also forces them off the optimal line on exit. Notice how greens entry and exit line are near parallel, where reds are over 180 requiring "backtracking" to get on line. this means green not only saves time through this corner, he will be faster and on a better line for the next.

Weight Balance

Weight transfer is where or how we load our suspension. This is directly related to line choice as well as throttle and braking control. If we look at the first valley in our lateral g graph, we notice greens slope is significantly smoother and more mellow than reds. The steepness of the slope is the rate at which we transition our weight into the corner. Sometimes this can still be a steep slope with an ideal lap but on a longer corner where we're testing max lateral gs, Transferring our weight for the corner sooner rather than later matters too. by getting our suspension set in before the corner, we don't get a sharp body roll while trying to max our lateral gs. This helps us preserve traction.

Throttle and Braking

Trail braking is the act of braking hard coming into the corner, then tapering off braking pressure as you reach the apex rather than braking entirely before. this lets you get the weight on your steering tires and prevents some risks of understeering on initial steer in, especially in rear-wheel drive cars. after trail braking we need to transition over to our throttle after the apex. This is a little complex for starting out, but the principles still apply.

  • Later apex: earlier throttle pick-up and better exit.
  • Smoother Lat-G: fewer spikes, higher average grip.

Overview

Turn 3 is our weirdest turn. it has approx. 15 feet of elevation change and a harsh grade reversal at the top. On this turn we actually see a reversal in trends from turn 5. Green sets up more inside and exits on the outside of the end of the turn. This is mainly to get over the crest at the top of the corner straighter. Turning too much causes the car to decompress and spin on throttle. Another reason for this is set up from the previous corner. Like the end of turn 5 into 6, we don't want to complicate our entry from turn 2 into 3. Green enters on the same trajectory as exit so he doesn't have to set up again and can shift his weight for the corner sooner.

Line Choice

Car set up was also a big thing on this corner. Red had way too high of a pressure in the tires. We see this in the data on our left increasing edge of our lateral g graph. The large spike up and down is the car losing traction and regaining it. we see this in the middle of the corner which also forced the line further outside.
